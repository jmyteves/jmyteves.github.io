Cell & Molecular Biologist

Joji Marie Teves, PhD

I study what signatures are encoded in cell's chromatin, profile the intermediate states when a cell's identity shift, and track these during dynamic processes such as tissue regeneration. I am using the endometrium, one of the body's most regenerative organs, as a system to understand this.

About

I am the daughter of two academics, born and raised in the Philippines. I've built my career across four countries, following a single thread of curiosity, how cells work, how they remember what they are, and how they rebuild.

It began with a fully funded US State Department scholarship to the University of Arizona, where I first worked with patient-derived cells. Wanting to do science for the public good, I returned to the Philippines and helped build the country's first biobank of gynecologic-cancer patients, to contribute in studying how endocrine-disrupting chemical exposure relates to disease. All of my pre-PhD work was with human disease models, but I kept wanting to understand how disease happens in the first place.

So in 2019 I started a PhD at the University of Copenhagen, which took me deep into chromatin biology and taught me genomics and bioinformatics. In the midst of my PhD, I wasn't sure what to do next. I wanted to explore the possibility of staying in academia and "try my luck". I applied for the Novo Nordisk Foundation fellowship for Postdocs abroad and was fortunate to have been chosen and awarded DKK 4,000,000 to pursue postdoctoral research at MIT where I worked to understand how cells forget their identity to adopt a new one, in a controlled way.

Two years into MIT, a personal constraint forced a career break, and a shifting political situation made returning impossible. Unsure of the next step, I walked the 800 km of the Camino de Santiago in the spring of 2025. Somewhere in the last 100 km, an unexpected conversation with a former colleague, now leading a group at IDIBELL and expanding into a tissue I'd worked with before my PhD, opened a door.Studying how tissues regenerate after damage, it turned out, helped me rebuild and put my life and career back on track.

When I pivoted into endometrial research, I was struck by how little we know about it. It is one of the most regenerative organs in the human body, and almost nobody is studying it. That gap is what I'm building on now.
